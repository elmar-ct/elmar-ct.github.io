Separation Axioms

As Rhoklin stated it well in Beginner's Course in Topology, there are more than \(10\) Separation Axioms or Trennungsaxiom. Here we are focusing on the essential ones. In all that follow, \(X\) is a topological space whose topology will be specified contextwisely.

\(T_0\): Kolmogorov

Given a pair of different points in \( X\), at least one of the two points has a neighborhood not containing the other.

In other words, in a \(T_0\) space, all points are distinguishable. From any pair of different points picked within the space, one can always isolate one of the points in such a way to exclude the other.

\(T_1\): Fréchet

Each point of any pair of different points in \(X\) possesses a neighborhood which does not contain the other.

The only difference between \(T_0\) and \(T_1\) is that, for \(T_1\), it is required that each one of the two different points has a neighborhood, -i.e., an open set not containing the other while in the case of \(T_0\), only one of them is required to have such a neighborhood. In particular, a \(T_1\) space is also a \(T_0\) one.

Equivalent formulation for \(T_1\): each point constitutes a closed set; therefore each finite set is closed too.

\(T_2\): Hausdorff

Two different points in \(X\) possess disjoint neighborhoods.

This is the most commonly used among the separation axioms. \(T_2\) spaces are the natural spaces for Analysis context since it allows one to take a limit without getting into a trouble(It guarantees the uniqueness of limits!)

What this says is that any two distinct points in \(X\) can be separated, distinguished, set apart one from another by open sets. In other words, given \(x\neq y\), there exist \(U(x)\) and \(U(y)\), neighborhoods to respectively \(x\) and \(y\) such that: \(U(x) \cap U(y) = \emptyset. \)

\(T_3\) and Regular Spaces

Any point and any closed set excluding this point have disjoint neighborhoods.

In other words, for any point \(x \in X\) and any closed set \(C\) excluding \(x\), there exists neighborhoods \(U(x)\) and \(U(C)\) of respectively \(x\) and \(C\) satisfying \(U(x) \cap U(C) = \emptyset. \)

Equivalent formulation: every neighborhood of an arbitrary point contains the closure of a neighborhood of this point. We will prove later why that is so.

If the axioms \(T_1\) and \(T_3\) are simultaneously fulfilled then the space \(X\) is said to be regular space.

\(T_4\) and Normal Spaces

Any two disjoint closed sets of \(X\) possess disjoint neighborhoods including them.

In other words, for any closed subsets \(C_1\) and \(C_2\), \(C_\cap C_2 = \emptyset\), there exist neighborhoods \(U(C_1)\) and \(U(C_2)\) such that \(C_1 \subset U(C_1)\), \(C_2 \subset U(C_2)\) and \(U(C_1) \cap U(C_2) = \emptyset\).

A topological space for which both the \(T_1\) and the \(T_4\) axioms are satisfied is said to be normal space.


We now prove now that that the two formulations for \( T_3 \)axiom are in fact equivalent. Assume the old, that is, the first version. Let \(x \in X\) be given and let \(U(y)\) be its neigborhood. The set \(X_x := X\setminus U(x) \) is closed, at the same time it excludes the point \(x\). There is therefore another neighborhood, denoted by \( O_x \) such that \(C_x \subseteq O_x \) and \( U(x) \cap O_x = \emptyset. \) Set \(V(x) := X\setminus O_x \). The set \(V(x)\) is obviously an open set containing \(x\). Moreover $$ \overline{V(x)} = \overline{X\setminus O_x} = X\setminus \operatorname{Int}(O_x) = X\setminus O_x \subseteq X\setminus C_x = U(x). $$

Conversely, let \(x\) be an arbitrary point and \(C\) an arbitrary closed set satisfying \(x\notin C\). Define \( U:= X\setminus C \). \(U\) is an open set containing \(x\), -i.e., a neighborhood of \(x\). By the new axiom, there exists another neighborhood \(V\) of \(x\), \(U\supseteq V\) such that \(\overline{V} \subset U\). Then, the set defined by \(O:= X\setminus \overline{V}\) is an open set satisfying $$ O = X\setminus \overline{V} \supseteq X\setminus U = C. $$ \tok Besides, it is immediate that \( V\cap O = V\cap \left( X\setminus V \right) = \emptyset. \) Hence, the open sets \(V\) and \(O\) separate the point \(x\) from the closed set \(C\) in the sense of the axiom \(T_3\).
